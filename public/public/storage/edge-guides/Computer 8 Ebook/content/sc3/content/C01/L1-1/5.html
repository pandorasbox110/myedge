Changes in States of Matter

Matter can change from one state to another. When a kind of matter is heated, its state changes. When heat is added to an object, its molecules move faster. The faster the molecules move, the more freely they spread apart.

For example, when butter, which is a solid, is placed in a cold pan, it maintains its shape. When heat is applied to it, its molecules spread apart and move faster. The butter turns liquid and takes the shape of the container where it is placed. When it is cooled, its molecules slow down and the butter becomes solid again.

On the other hand, in a glass of water, the molecules slide over each other. When the glass is placed in a freezer, the molecules of the water slow down and the water turns into ice, which is a solid. When the ice is removed from the freezer and placed on a table at room temperature, it will melt and become a liquid. Then when it is heated, it evaporates in a form of gas called water vapor. When the heat is removed from the water vapor and the temperature cools down, it changes back into a liquid.

A very good example of the changes in states of matter is seen in nature, in the water cycle. In this cycle, when heat from the sun strikes a water surface, the water evaporates to form water vapor. When it cools, it forms into small droplets and falls on the ground in the form of rain. In certain countries, when the temperature becomes very low, the droplets solidify and fall to the ground in the form of snow, which is a solid.
